# Service System and Delivery Operating Model for an AI Implementation Consultancy

## Service model and fit

**SECTION 1 — RECOMMENDED SERVICE MODEL**

The strongest best-practice service model for this specific consultancy is a **staged, implementation-led program** that begins with a tightly scoped **Operational Diagnostic** (to create certainty), immediately transitions into a **Quick-Win Delivery Sprint** (to create visible progress fast), and then moves into repeatable **Implementation Pods** (to scale outcomes across workflows and functions). This model is “productized” through standardized artifacts, gates, and cadences—while still allowing customization at the solution level.

This recommendation is grounded in well-established delivery and risk patterns:

- **Trust and governance must be built into AI work** (not stapled on later). Risk and trustworthiness are explicitly framed as lifecycle concerns in the entity["organization","NIST","us standards agency"] AI Risk Management Framework (Govern/Map/Measure/Manage). citeturn0search0turn0search4turn0search28  
- **Repeatable delivery requires operational rigor** (secure SDLC practices, test gates, documentation, monitoring). NIST’s Secure Software Development Framework (SSDF) describes a core set of secure development practices intended to be integrated into any SDLC. citeturn4search0turn4search12turn4search4  
- **“Automation first” is not a slogan; it is a feasibility and ROI reality**. RPA/standard automation tends to work best when processes are high-volume, standardized, rules-based, mature, and stable, with clear costs/value—making it a logical early layer before adding AI complexity. citeturn2search21turn2search13turn2search3  
- **Time-to-value improves when sequencing is economic, not political**. Weighted Shortest Job First (WSJF) prioritizes work by relative cost of delay divided by duration, a widely used economic sequencing logic in scaled delivery systems. citeturn0search3turn0search23  
- **Adoption is a delivery requirement, not a “nice-to-have.”** The entity["company","Prosci","change management firm"] ADKAR model frames successful change as individuals reaching Awareness/Desire/Knowledge/Ability/Reinforcement—useful as an operational checklist for adoption readiness. citeturn1search0  

### What the “core service” actually is

The core service should be positioned and run as a **Business-Outcome Implementation System**:

- You don’t sell “AI consulting.”  
- You sell **measurable operational improvement**, delivered through a disciplined path:
  - Diagnose how work and systems behave today  
  - Build a prioritized ROI backlog  
  - Implement the fastest, safest improvements first (often standard automation/software)  
  - Add AI only where it materially improves results  
  - Train, measure adoption, and expand

This aligns with both AI lifecycle discipline (governance, measurement, monitoring) and practical operations improvement methods (process mapping, waste identification, bottleneck removal). citeturn0search0turn1search2turn1search3  

### Recommended staged structure (single model, repeatable sequence)

**Stage A — Operational Diagnostic (productized)**
- Purpose: create certainty, baseline metrics, and a prioritized backlog.  
- Output: “ROI-ranked Opportunity Backlog + Implementation Roadmap + Quick-Win Candidate.”  
- Why it sells: clearly bounded scope and a tangible asset delivered to the client.

**Stage B — Quick-Win Delivery Sprint (productized)**
- Purpose: ship one visible improvement ASAP (often automation/integration/reporting).  
- Output: “Live improvement in production + training + measurement.”  
- Why it sells: reduces perceived risk and accelerates trust.

**Stage C — Implementation Pods (modular, repeatable)**
- Purpose: execute backlog items in small batches (2–6 weeks per pod).  
- Output: delivered workflows, adoption support, and measured ROI.

**Stage D — Scale & Governance (optional retainer / managed support)**
- Purpose: keep improvements alive (monitoring, iteration, new opportunities, governance).  
- Output: continuous pipeline of improvements + lightweight AI governance.

This staged model mirrors how mature delivery organizations reduce risk: map/measure early, deploy in small increments, and operationalize via repeatable lifecycle practices. citeturn2search1turn2search4turn2search2  

### Scope boundaries (what makes this easier to sell and fulfill)

A premium, repeatable offer benefits from explicit boundaries:

- **Bound the Diagnostic:** 1–2 value streams (e.g., “order-to-cash” or “ticket-to-resolution”), specific teams, defined data sources, and a fixed timeframe. Tools like SIPOC provide a high-level “as-is” scope view to prevent discovery from turning into endless interviews. citeturn1search2turn1search14  
- **Bound the Quick Win:** 1 production release (or 1 workflow) + training + measurement.  
- **Make Implementation Pods modular:** each pod has a clearly defined “done” that includes adoption instrumentation and a measurable outcome.

### What should be standardized vs customized

Standardize anything that reduces friction, increases certainty, and improves quality:

- Intake and onboarding
- Discovery scripts and process scan methodology
- Process mapping format (SIPOC + BPMN where needed)
- KPI baseline method and measurement plan
- Prioritization scoring model (WSJF-derived + feasibility gates)
- Solution design templates and review gates
- Testing and “definition of done”
- Training format and adoption tracking
- Reporting cadence and artifacts
- Security and risk checks (SSDF-inspired) citeturn4search0turn4search12  

Customize where the client’s context actually matters:

- Specific bottleneck root causes  
- Data structures and system integration constraints  
- Workflow UI/UX and change management approaches  
- The choice of automation vs AI techniques (based on readiness and value)

### Which clients this model is best for

This model is strongest for organizations that:
- Have meaningful operational volume (repetition = ROI)  
- Have at least some digital systems producing logs/data (enables faster scan and measurement)  
- Can designate accountable process owners and data owners  
- Want outcomes, not experiments

Process mining and task mining—using system event logs and user interaction data—are examples of approaches organizations use to reconstruct how work actually runs, which improves diagnostic accuracy and speeds prioritization. citeturn4search6turn4search11turn4search27  

**SECTION 2 — IDEAL CLIENT AND FIT**

### Best-fit client profile

A best-fit client for this service system typically has:

- **Clear operational pain** (manual work, rework, delays, bottlenecks, inconsistent KPIs). Lean value-stream mapping is explicitly designed to surface waste and improvement opportunities across end-to-end flows, which aligns with your “scan → identify waste → prioritize → implement” sequence. citeturn1search3turn1search23  
- **An executive sponsor + an operational owner** willing to set priorities and remove blockers (critical to speed and certainty).
- **Accessible systems and data** (even if imperfect). The goal is not perfect data; the goal is to know what is feasible now, what needs a foundation first, and what should wait.
- **A willingness to standardize basic workflow steps** before demanding AI.

### Not a fit (or only a fit under strict conditions)

- Firms seeking a **pure “AI transformation” narrative** without operational rigor and measurement. The risk is overbuilding and under-adopting.
- Firms with **no process ownership** (“everyone owns it”)—you will not get stable decisions.
- Firms that cannot provide **minimum data access** or **security approvals** required to implement anything.
- Firms expecting a vendor to “just build it” with **no change management**—adoption fails.

### Conditions required for success

- A named **Accountable** business owner for each workflow. (Role clarity is a standard project control mechanism; RACI is widely used to clarify Responsible/Accountable/Consulted/Informed.) citeturn3search23turn3search27  
- A baseline measurement plan and agreement on what constitutes success (trust depends on measurement).  
- A commitment to short feedback loops: frequent reviews and safe incremental releases (a core MLOps/DevOps operationalization theme is continuous delivery/automation and feedback). citeturn2search1turn2search4  

## External client delivery process

**SECTION 3 — EXTERNAL CLIENT DELIVERY PROCESS**

This is the **exact client journey**, designed for clarity, trust, and fast visible progress. It respects your required sequencing: diagnose work → map manual activity → review KPIs/data → prioritize by ROI → implement standard automation first → add AI only where it materially improves results.

### Delivery principle: diagnose with evidence, deliver in small batches

- **Workflow truth over opinions:** use interviews + artifacts + logs where possible. Process mining uses event logs to show how processes actually run; task mining uses user interaction data to reveal what people actually do at the desktop layer. citeturn4search6turn4search11turn4search27  
- **Visible progress early:** align with change guidance that emphasizes generating short-term wins to sustain momentum (a core element in the entity["people","John Kotter","change leadership author"] 8-step change approach). citeturn3search6  
- **Trust through measurement:** define “done” as “working + adopted + measured,” not “built.”

### Stage by stage process (purchase → outcome → expansion)

#### Stage one: Delivery onboarding and alignment

**Objective**  
Create certainty, reduce friction, and establish the operating rhythm.

**Inputs required**  
Signed SOW, initial goals, stakeholder list, access requirements, current KPIs (even if imperfect).

**Outputs / deliverables**  
- Project charter (scope, goals, success measures, constraints)  
- Communication plan (cadence, channels, stakeholders)  
- Access checklist + security intake  
- Delivery calendar (workshops + review gates)

Structured communication plans define who needs what information, when, and how—reducing confusion and missed expectations. citeturn5search3turn5search12turn5search6  

**Decision made at this stage**  
Confirm “engagement boundaries” (what is in/out) and success metrics.

**Who is involved**  
Client sponsor, process owner(s), IT/security contact, consultancy engagement lead.

**What the client sees**  
A single, clear “how we will work” packet and schedule.

**What creates trust here**  
A visible plan, known owners, and agreed success metrics.

**Quick win here**  
Rapid setup + clarity: client gets one page showing the entire path and what happens next.

**What could go wrong if weak**  
Scope creep, access delays, unclear expectations.

---

#### Stage two: Discovery and workflow scan

**Objective**  
Understand how teams work today, and identify where time and outcomes are lost.

**Inputs required**  
SME interviews, existing SOPs, sample tickets/cases, system list, screenshots, outputs, forms, spreadsheets.

**Outputs / deliverables**  
- SIPOC for each target workflow (high-level boundary and stakeholders). SIPOC is defined as capturing suppliers/inputs/process/outputs/customers to scope and understand processes. citeturn1search2turn1search14  
- “As-is” workflow map (high level) and candidate sub-processes for deeper mapping.

**Decision made at this stage**  
Which 1–3 workflows become the diagnostic focus (and what is explicitly excluded).

**Who is involved**  
Process owner, 2–4 SMEs, IT liaison, consultancy process analyst.

**What the client sees**  
A credible picture of “how work flows” with boundaries and pain points.

**What creates trust**  
Maps that match reality (validated with SMEs) rather than abstract theory.

**Quick win**  
A “top 10 friction list” surfaced and validated.

**What could go wrong if weak**  
You’ll optimize the wrong workflow (“fixing the wrong problem well”).

---

#### Stage three: Role/activity mapping and manual-task identification

**Objective**  
Quantify manual effort and identify repeatable tasks.

**Inputs required**  
Time estimates, task steps, exceptions list, volumes, rework rates.

**Outputs / deliverables**  
- Role → activity matrix  
- “Manual Task Register” (task, frequency, time spent, pain level, error risk, systems touched)  
- Candidate automation opportunities

If available, task mining can provide granular visibility into user-level actions (clicks/keystrokes/data entries) to assess task efficiency within a broader process. citeturn4search11  

**Decision made**  
Which manual tasks are “automation-ready” now vs require process redesign first.

**Who is involved**  
SMEs, process owner, consultancy analyst + automation lead.

**What the client sees**  
A prioritized list of real tasks—not vague “AI opportunities.”

**Trust**  
Effort and pain are quantified with evidence (samples/logs where possible).

**Quick win**  
Identify one “low-change, high-frequency” task for early automation.

**What could go wrong**  
You select tasks with high exception rates or unstable inputs (poor automation fit). RPA fit is strongest when processes are rule-based, stable, and low-exception. citeturn2search21turn2search13  

---

#### Stage four: KPI and data review

**Objective**  
Tie workflow pain to business outcomes and verify data readiness.

**Inputs required**  
KPI definitions, reports/dashboards, relevant datasets, data owners, data dictionary if available.

**Outputs / deliverables**  
- KPI baseline snapshot (current state)  
- Data readiness notes (quality, completeness, access, governance)  
- Measurement plan: “how we will prove change worked”

AI and analytics delivery lifecycles emphasize spending substantial effort in data understanding and preparation; IBM notes that data understanding is critical to avoid unexpected problems in downstream phases. citeturn2search27  

**Decision made**  
What can be measured now, and what requires instrumentation first.

**Who is involved**  
Client data owner, operations owner, consultancy data lead.

**What the client sees**  
A clear baseline and “what we can prove.”

**Trust**  
Shared agreement on metrics and measurement.

**Quick win**  
Instrument 1–2 fast “progress indicators” (e.g., cycle time, backlog age).

**What could go wrong**  
“Success” becomes subjective; stakeholders lose confidence.

---

#### Stage five: Bottleneck identification and root-cause hypotheses

**Objective**  
Identify where flow breaks and why.

**Inputs required**  
Workflow maps, task register, KPI baseline, exceptions list.

**Outputs / deliverables**  
- Bottleneck map (hand-offs, queues, approvals, rework loops)  
- Root-cause hypotheses and validation plan

Value-stream mapping is used to diagram steps and flows and identify waste; it is widely used to locate bottlenecks and inefficiency across entire streams. citeturn1search3turn1search23turn1search31  

**Decision made**  
Which bottlenecks will be attacked first (based on ROI + speed).

**Who is involved**  
Ops owner, SMEs, consultancy team.

**What the client sees**  
A “here is why it’s slow” narrative backed by evidence.

**Trust**  
Your diagnosis matches lived reality and data.

**Quick win**  
Identify one bottleneck solvable without deep change (e.g., eliminate duplicate entry).

**What could go wrong**  
You treat symptoms (automation) without fixing root constraints.

---

#### Stage six: Prioritization workshop and ROI backlog creation

**Objective**  
Turn findings into a ranked, gated delivery backlog.

**Inputs required**  
Opportunity list, estimated benefits, effort estimates, feasibility notes, risks.

**Outputs / deliverables**  
- Prioritized “Opportunity Backlog”  
- Implementation roadmap (30/60/90 day plan)  
- Quick-win selection and success metrics

WSJF provides a widely used method to sequence work by cost of delay divided by job duration, making prioritization more economic and transparent. citeturn0search3turn0search23  

**Decision made**  
- What gets built next  
- What is postponed  
- What is explicitly not in scope now

**Who is involved**  
Sponsor, process owners, IT, consultancy lead.

**What the client sees**  
A ranked plan with clear “why this first.”

**Trust**  
Transparent scoring and explicit tradeoffs.

**Quick win**  
Lock the quick-win sprint scope on the call.

**What could go wrong**  
Politics overrides economics; you pick “important” but slow items first.

---

#### Stage seven: Automation-before-AI decision gate

**Objective**  
Choose the simplest solution that produces the outcome.

**Inputs required**  
Task profiles, data readiness, risk level, change management needs.

**Outputs / deliverables**  
A per-item decision:  
- Standard automation/software change  
- Data foundation work  
- AI augmentation (only if it materially improves outcome)

RPA suitability research and vendor guidance repeatedly emphasize that early automation success correlates with rule-based, standardized, stable, high-volume processes (low exception, structured data). citeturn2search21turn2search3turn2search13  

**Decision made**  
Proceed as automation, AI, or defer pending foundation.

**What the client sees**  
A principled “why automation first / why AI now.”

**Trust**  
Clients see you are not forcing AI into weak foundations.

**Quick win**  
Many items will “downgrade” to simpler automation—faster value.

**What could go wrong**  
AI gets used where standard automation is safer and faster.

#### Stage eight: Implementation planning and solution design

**Objective**  
Finalize scope, architecture, test plan, training plan, and release plan.

**Inputs required**  
Backlog item definition, system constraints, user roles, data access.

**Outputs / deliverables**  
- Solution design doc (lightweight, decision-focused)  
- Implementation plan (milestones, owners, risks)  
- Test plan + acceptance criteria  
- Rollout plan + training plan

Sign-off to build (and what requires approval/security review).

**Who is involved**  
Client IT/security, process owner, consultancy architect and build lead.

**Trust**  
Clear acceptance criteria and a rollback approach.

**Quick win**  
A clickable prototype / sample output / demo flow before full build.

**What could go wrong**  
Ambiguous requirements → rebuild cycles and delays.

**Objective**

**Inputs required**  
Dev environment, data access, integration keys, user test group.

**Outputs / deliverables**  
- Working automation and/or AI augmentation

**What the client sees**

Small batches, frequent demos, and visible quality gates.

**Quick win**  
Deliver “thin slice” functionality into production quickly.

**What could go wrong**  
Integrations break; quality issues reduce confidence.

Prove it works and it’s safe.

## Decision systems

**SECTION 5 — PRIORITIZATION FRAMEWORK**

The goal is to prioritize:
- highest business outcome
- fastest visible progress
- lowest delivery risk
- best foundation-building for future improvements

### Define a two-layer prioritization system (gates + scoring)

**Layer 1: Hard gates (must pass before an item can be prioritized highly)**  
An opportunity cannot be “top priority” if any of the following is true:

- No clear owner (Accountable)  
- Not measurable (no KPI or proxy available)  
- Requires a major upstream process redesign not yet agreed  
- Required data access is blocked for the next sprint/pod  
- Implementation depends on another major platform decision

**Layer 2: Economic scoring (rank what remains)**  
Use a WSJF-derived approach plus feasibility modifiers.

WSJF is defined as relative cost of delay divided by relative job duration (or size). citeturn0search3turn0search23  

### Practical scoring model (implementation-ready)

Score each opportunity on a 1–5 scale for each factor:

**A. Outcome value**
- Business impact (revenue, cost, risk reduction, service level)
- KPI leverage (how directly it moves a key metric)

**B. Delay cost**
- Time criticality (deadline, churn risk, regulatory risk)

**C. Effort and speed**
- Implementation speed (time to first measurable result)
- Complexity (systems, integrations, exception handling)
- Change management load (training + behavior change)

**D. Evidence and readiness**
- Data quality and access readiness
- Workflow stability (how often it changes)
- Exception rate (high exception = lower early priority)

**E. Solution path fit**
- Suitability for standard automation
- Suitability for AI augmentation (only if needed)

Compute:

**Priority Score = (Cost of Delay × Confidence) / Job Size**

Where:
- **Cost of Delay = (Business Impact + Time Criticality + Risk Reduction/Opportunity Enablement)** (1–5 each)  
- **Job Size = (Effort + Complexity + Change Load)** (1–5 each)  
- **Confidence = (Evidence Quality + Data Readiness)/2** scaled to 0.6–1.2

This retains the core WSJF concept while explicitly modeling uncertainty (a major killer of AI/automation programs). citeturn0search3turn0search23  

### Clear decision sequence (what gets fixed first vs postponed)

1) **Fix measurement first** (if you cannot prove improvement, you cannot sustain trust).  
2) **Fix high-frequency, low-exception manual work** (best chance of a quick win).  
3) **Fix bottlenecks that constrain throughput** (queues, approvals, handoffs).  
4) **Fix data/definition issues that unlock multiple improvements** (foundation work).  
5) **Only then pursue complex AI augmentation** (when foundations exist).

**What gets postponed**
- High-complexity items with low evidence  
- Items requiring major org redesign  
- Items where AI value depends on data not yet available

**What should never be prioritized early**
- Full “AI copilots” across messy workflows without baseline and governance  
- Replacing human judgment in high-risk contexts before proving reliability and adoption

**SECTION 6 — AUTOMATION-BEFORE-AI DECISION MODEL**

The decision rule: **Prefer the simplest approach that reliably produces the desired outcome with the least operational and governance burden.**

### Stepwise decision framework (practical, repeatable)

#### Step one: Can standardization remove the problem without building anything?
If the workflow fails due to inconsistent definitions, unclear ownership, or policy ambiguity, fix that first.

#### Step two: Can “standard automation” solve it reliably?
Standard automation includes: integration, workflow rules, forms, alerts, RPA, scheduled jobs, reporting.

RPA/automation fit is strongest where work is rule-based, standardized, mature, and stable with clear costs/value. citeturn2search21turn2search13turn2search3  

If **YES**, implement automation first.  
If **NO**, proceed.

#### Step three: Does the task require cognitive interpretation of unstructured input?
Examples: email triage, document classification, summarization, extracting entities from text, drafting responses.  
This is where AI *might* add value—*after* guardrails and evaluation exist.

#### Step four: Is data and evaluation readiness sufficient?
If you cannot define:
- what “good” output is,
- how you’ll test it,
- and how you’ll monitor it,
then it should not ship as AI.

MLOps best practices emphasize continuous delivery/automation plus monitoring and lifecycle management for ML systems. citeturn2search1turn2search4  

#### Step five: Is risk manageable with controls?
For LLM-enabled systems, maintain explicit threat checks aligned to OWASP LLM Top 10 categories (prompt injection, sensitive information disclosure, excessive agency, etc.). citeturn0search2turn0search18  

If risk is high, require:
- human-in-the-loop approvals
- restricted tool access
- audit logs
- safe fallback behavior

#### Step six: Choose AI pattern (augmentation before autonomy)
Start with:
- assistive AI (drafts, suggestions, classification)
- constrained actions (templates, validation)
Only later consider agentic autonomy, and only with strict governance and monitoring.

### Output artifact: the “Automation/AI Decision Record”
For every backlog item above a threshold size, produce a one-page decision record:
- desired outcome
- automation option(s) considered
- AI option(s) considered
- chosen approach + why
- risks and mitigations
- measurement plan
- “rollback path”

Decision records/decision logs are used to preserve context, alternatives, and rationale—reducing confusion later. citeturn5search10turn5search25

**Risk**: “AI consulting” becomes vague; client expects transformation while budget covers a sprint.

- tight stage boundaries and explicit exclusions in the charter
- milestone gates with sign-offs

**SECTION 10 — EXPANSION MODEL**

Expansion should feel like the natural next step, not a new sales pitch. The best-practice sequence is to expand along **three dimensions**—depth, breadth, and capability.

### Depth expansion (same workflow, deeper improvement)
1) Automation of highest-frequency manual steps  
2) Integration/data fixes that remove rework  
3) Exception handling improvements  
4) AI augmentation only when it materially improves outcomes and is governable

### Breadth expansion (adjacent workflows in the same value stream)
After one workflow proves value, expand to upstream/downstream steps:
- intake → processing → exception management → reporting

Value stream mapping is designed to consider end-to-end actions and flows, making it a natural guide for expansion sequencing across a value stream. citeturn1search3turn1search23  

### Cross-functional expansion (repeat the system in other functions)
Replicate the same staged model:
- diagnostic → quick win → pods → governance

Process mining can support cross-functional scaling by using event logs to reconstruct how processes run across systems, improving targeting and measurement. citeturn4search6turn4search2  

### Advisory / retainer support (Scale & Governance)
Move into a lightweight operating cadence:
- monthly backlog refresh + prioritization  
- quarterly governance and risk review  
- ongoing monitoring, model/automation health checks  
- enablement refresh and onboarding of new users

For AI governance alignment, ISO/IEC 42001 specifies requirements and guidance for establishing and continually improving an AI management system, which can inform a scaled governance offering. citeturn0search1turn0search17  

**SECTION 11 — FINAL RECOMMENDATION**

### The strongest overall system to build

Build a single system with four layers:

**Layer one: A productized entry diagnostic (sell first)**
- “Operational Diagnostic + ROI Backlog + Quick-Win Selection”  
Standardize: intake, mapping, measurement, scoring, reporting.  
Flex: which workflows and KPIs.

**Layer two: A guaranteed quick-win sprint (deliver second)**
- One production go-live with training and measurable movement.  
Standardize: delivery cadence, test gates, release checklist, adoption playbook.  
Flex: the implementation method (integration, automation, UI, reporting).

**Layer three: Implementation pods (scale third)**
- Repeatable 2–6 week pods that deliver backlog items.  
Standardize: pod structure, QA, documentation, communication, adoption measurement.  
Flex: solution design per client context.

**Layer four: Scale & Governance retainer (add later)**
- Ongoing optimization + governance + capability building.  
Standardize: monthly/quarterly cadences, dashboards, risk checks.  
Flex: depth of governance (based on client risk profile).

### What should be built first (in sequence)

1) **Templates and gates** (charter, mapping, KPI baseline, prioritization, decision record, test plan, go-live checklist, outcome report)  
2) **A repeatable diagnostic workflow** (SIPOC + task register + KPI baseline + backlog scoring) citeturn1search2turn0search3  
3) **Quick-win delivery mechanics** (release process, training, adoption telemetry)  
4) **Automation-first patterns** (top 10 standard automations/integrations you can reuse)  
5) **AI augmentation patterns** only after you’ve proven the delivery engine and measurement discipline (and can apply OWASP LLM risk checks when applicable) citeturn0search2turn2search1  

### What should be standardized first vs kept flexible

**Standardize first**
- engagement cadence and artifacts  
- prioritization scoring  
- QA and security gates (SSDF-driven discipline for software; OWASP checks for LLM items) citeturn4search12turn0search2  
- reporting formats and decision logs citeturn5search10turn5search21  

**Keep flexible**
- which workflows are in scope  
- which tools/platforms are used (fit to client ecosystem)  
- how much change management intensity is required

### What should be sold first vs added later

**Sell first**
- Diagnostic + Quick Win (reduces buyer uncertainty and accelerates value)

**Add later**
- AI-heavy initiatives (only after foundational automation, measurement, and governance demonstrate success)
- long-term scale/governance retainers (only after you have an outcomes track record inside the client)
