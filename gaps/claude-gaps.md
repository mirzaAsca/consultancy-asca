# Business Idea Destruction Report

## MARKET FAILURE POINTS

### 1. The "AI consulting" market is already saturated and collapsing in credibility

**Evidence:** Every Big 4 firm (Deloitte, McKinsey, BCG, Accenture) has an AI transformation practice. Every boutique consulting firm has pivoted to "AI strategy." The market is flooded with people promising measurable AI ROI. PwC, which you cite in your own research, has thousands of consultants doing exactly this. Your document even references their 2026 finding that "most CEOs report no tangible AI returns" — which means the existing providers are failing, and that poisons the well for everyone, including you.

**Kill probability:** High. Not because your approach is wrong, but because buyer fatigue and skepticism toward "AI transformation" is at an all-time high. Every failed engagement from Accenture or McKinsey makes your sale harder, not easier.

**To overcome:** You need a radically different positioning that distances you from "AI consulting" entirely. Your document actually hints at this ("not AI consulting but measurable operational improvement") but the calculator, the language, and the service architecture all still scream AI consultancy.

### 2. The sweet spot segment (10-150 employees) doesn't have budget for this

**Evidence:** Your Constraint Sprint path starts with a $5-15K diagnostic, then moves into implementation pods. A 30-person company doing $30M in revenue sounds rich on paper, but these companies are typically founder-led, capital-efficient by necessity, and allergic to recurring consulting fees. $22K/month is $264K/year — that's 2-3 full-time senior hires. These founders will do the math and say "I'll just hire an AI engineer."

**Kill probability:** High for volume. You might close a few, but the segment is structurally resistant to retained consulting relationships. They buy tools, not advisors.

**To overcome:** You'd need a radically different pricing model for this segment — project-based with hard deliverables and completion dates, not monthly retainers. Your document still frames Plan A as $22K/mo, which is a retainer. Sweet spot founders will bounce.

### 3. The problem isn't painful enough at the Constraint Sprint level

**Evidence:** Your ideal Constraint Sprint buyer has a "clear operational bottleneck" — but if it were truly painful, they'd already be solving it. Companies with 30 people and $30M revenue are succeeding *despite* the bottleneck. The urgency isn't there. They'll take the free calculator, nod at the results, and do nothing for 18 months. Your document acknowledges this indirectly by designing a "Not Now" route, but the real risk is that 70%+ of Constraint Sprint-routed companies behave like "Not Now" companies.

**Kill probability:** Medium-high. The bottleneck exists, but the pain isn't acute enough to trigger a $264K/year purchase decision. These companies tolerate inefficiency remarkably well.

**To overcome:** You need a triggering event strategy — target companies during hiring freezes, post-layoff, fundraise pressure, or board mandate. The calculator alone won't create urgency.

### 4. Enterprise buyers don't buy from solo consultants or small firms for $35K+/month retained engagements

**Evidence:** Your Transformation Office tier targets 500-100,000+ employee companies at $35K+/month. These companies have procurement departments, vendor risk assessments, insurance requirements (E&O, cyber liability), SOC 2 expectations, and approved vendor lists. They buy from firms with brand recognition, reference clients in their industry, and teams of 10+ consultants. A single consultant with a calculator website will not pass the vendor qualification stage, regardless of how good the methodology is.

**Kill probability:** Very high for the enterprise segment as currently conceived. You will not close a $420K+/year retained engagement with a Fortune 500 company without a team, a brand, and reference clients they can call.

**To overcome:** Start with the Constraint Sprint segment exclusively. Build case studies. Build a team. Then move upmarket in 2-3 years. Trying to serve both segments from day one is a classic consulting firm mistake.

## EXECUTION FAILURE POINTS

### 5. You're building a 6-phase product roadmap for a business with zero customers

**Evidence:** Your document describes 6 implementation phases, 8 test fixtures, bottleneck playbooks for 5 functions, industry overlays for 5+ verticals, two complete service delivery systems, and a calculator routing engine — before you've had a single sales conversation. This is classic over-engineering. You're building the machine before you know if anyone wants what it produces.

**Kill probability:** Medium-high. Not because the plan is bad, but because you'll spend 3-6 months building instead of selling. By the time the calculator is "right," your cash runway is shorter and you still have no validation that the routing actually converts.

**To overcome:** Ship Phase 0-1 in 2 weeks max. Get 10 companies through the calculator. Talk to every one of them. Adjust based on what they actually say, not what your document predicts they'll say.

### 6. Delivery capacity is a single point of failure

**Evidence:** This document describes two service lines, each with multi-month engagements, governance systems, implementation pods, steering committees, and adoption tracking. Who delivers all of this? If it's you alone, you can serve maybe 2-3 clients simultaneously. If you need to hire, you need revenue first. This is the classic consulting chicken-and-egg problem.

**Kill probability:** Medium. It won't kill the business, but it will cap your growth at solo-consultant income ($300-500K/year) for much longer than you expect.

**To overcome:** Design the delivery model around leverage — templates, frameworks, automation, and subcontractors — before you need them. Your document mentions "shared infrastructure across both lanes" but doesn't address who actually does the work.

### 7. The skills gap: sales to enterprise buyers

**Evidence:** Your document is analytically excellent — Hormozi frameworks, research citations, value equations. But enterprise sales is a relationship game. It requires warm introductions, 3-6 month sales cycles, multiple stakeholder management, procurement navigation, and RFP responses. None of this is addressable by a better calculator. The document focuses almost entirely on inbound lead generation and says nothing about outbound sales, partnerships, channel strategy, or how you actually get in front of a VP of Operations at a 2,000-person company.

**Kill probability:** High for the enterprise segment. Medium for Constraint Sprint (shorter sales cycle, founder-direct).

**To overcome:** Forget enterprise for now. Focus all energy on founder-led companies where you can sell direct in 1-2 conversations. Build the enterprise motion when you have a team and a brand.

## COMPETITION FAILURE POINTS

### 8. Existing solutions are good enough — and cheaper

**Evidence:** For the Constraint Sprint segment: companies can hire freelance AI engineers on Upwork for $100-200/hour, use tools like Zapier/Make/n8n for automation, or subscribe to vertical SaaS that already solves their specific bottleneck. A 30-person SaaS company with an engineering bottleneck is more likely to adopt Cursor, Copilot, or Linear's AI features than hire a consultant at $22K/month. For the enterprise segment: McKinsey, BCG, Deloitte, and Accenture already offer exactly what you describe, with bigger teams and brand credibility.

**Kill probability:** High. The "automation before AI" principle in your own document (from Doc 3) actually argues against your own service — most of these bottlenecks can be solved with tools, not consultants.

**To overcome:** You need to articulate why a generalist implementation consultant is better than (a) specialized SaaS tools and (b) an in-house hire. Your document doesn't make this case clearly.

### 9. The calculator itself is not a moat

**Evidence:** Any developer can build an ROI calculator in a weekend. The routing logic you describe is clever but trivially replicable. AI consultancies already have ROI calculators, assessment tools, and maturity scorecards. Your differentiator cannot be the calculator — it's a commodity lead-gen tactic.

**Kill probability:** Low in isolation, but it means you're investing significant engineering effort in something that provides zero competitive advantage. Every hour spent perfecting the calculator is an hour not spent on the actual differentiator (delivery methodology, case studies, relationships).

**To overcome:** Treat the calculator as a minimum viable lead magnet. Don't over-invest. Your real moat will be delivery track record and client outcomes.

### 10. The Hormozi framework is now table stakes

**Evidence:** "100M Offers" has sold millions of copies. Every online business builder has read it. Your prospects have read it. When they see a value equation, Grand Slam offer structure, and money model, they'll recognize the framework and may perceive you as derivative rather than original. "Free scorecard → paid diagnostic → retained engagement" is the exact funnel template taught in every Hormozi-adjacent course.

**Kill probability:** Low, but it undermines positioning. You're using the same playbook as thousands of other consultants, which makes you look like every other Hormozi-influenced service business.

**To overcome:** Use the frameworks internally but don't let them define your external positioning. The client doesn't care about your value equation math — they care about their specific problem being solved.

## BUSINESS MODEL FAILURE POINTS

### 11. Customer acquisition cost will eat your margins

**Evidence:** Your funnel is: content/SEO → calculator → waitlist form → diagnostic sale → retained engagement. Each step has conversion drop-off. Realistic numbers: 1,000 calculator visitors → 50 complete it → 10 submit the form → 3 book a call → 1 buys the diagnostic → 0.5 convert to retained. That's a 0.05% visitor-to-retained conversion rate. If you're paying for traffic (LinkedIn ads, content production), your CAC could easily be $10-20K+ per retained client. On a $22K/mo engagement, that's acceptable but tight. On a $5K diagnostic that doesn't convert, you're underwater.

**Kill probability:** Medium. Consultancy economics can work if close rates are high, but you're designing for inbound-only with no outbound motion. Inbound is slow and expensive to build.

**To overcome:** Supplement the calculator funnel with direct outreach, partnerships, and referrals. The calculator should be one channel, not the channel.

### 12. The diagnostic-to-retained conversion is where the model breaks

**Evidence:** You're designing a $5-15K paid diagnostic as the entry point. This is smart in theory (Hormozi's paid entry), but in practice, many companies will buy the diagnostic, receive the findings, and then implement internally or hire cheaper help. The diagnostic gives them the roadmap — they don't need you to execute it. This is the classic consulting leak: the audit sells, the implementation churns.

**Kill probability:** Medium-high. If your diagnostic-to-retained conversion is below 40%, the economics of the diagnostic pricing don't work and you're essentially doing cheap strategy work that subsidizes your competitors' delivery.

**To overcome:** Make the diagnostic output dependent on your delivery methodology. Don't hand over a generic roadmap — deliver findings that are actionable only within your engagement framework. Or: price the diagnostic higher and treat it as a standalone product.

### 13. Retention on retained engagements is structurally weak

**Evidence:** Your Constraint Sprint is designed to "remove the bottleneck." Once removed, why does the client keep paying $22K/month? Your continuity offer is a "Scale & Governance retainer" — but a 30-person company doesn't need ongoing governance. They needed one thing fixed. You fixed it. They cancel. For enterprise, the Transformation Office has better retention characteristics, but you're unlikely to land those clients (see point 4).

**Kill probability:** Medium. If average engagement length is 4-6 months instead of 12+, your LTV drops dramatically and the CAC math gets much harder.

**To overcome:** Build expansion into the delivery model — scope the first engagement narrowly, demonstrate value, then expand to adjacent workflows. But be honest: many Constraint Sprint clients will be one-and-done.

### 14. You're designing two businesses, not one

**Evidence:** The Transformation Office and Constraint Sprint are fundamentally different businesses. Different buyers, different sales cycles, different pricing, different delivery models, different team requirements, different marketing channels. Your document treats them as "routes" in one calculator, but operationally they require completely separate go-to-market motions. Trying to build both simultaneously with solo resources is a recipe for being mediocre at both.

**Kill probability:** Medium-high. Spreading focus across two distinct service lines before validating either one is a common consulting firm failure mode.

**To overcome:** Pick one. Ship it. Validate it. Then expand. Your document even quotes Hormozi: "Perfect one offer at a time." Then it proceeds to design two offers simultaneously.

## The meta-failure: this document is a procrastination artifact

The most dangerous thing about this 1,200-line guide is that writing it *feels* like progress. It's thorough, well-researched, and analytically rigorous. It's also a substitute for the only thing that matters right now: **talking to 20 potential buyers and finding out if any of them would pay you money.**

You have zero customers, zero case studies, zero validated pricing, and zero evidence that the routing logic maps to actual buying behavior. Every assumption in this document — the route thresholds, the pricing tiers, the KPI language, the bottleneck playbooks — is a hypothesis. Treat them as hypotheses, not architecture.

**The single highest-ROI action:** Stop building. Pick 20 companies that fit the Constraint Sprint profile. Reach out to the founders directly. Offer the diagnostic at whatever price they'll pay. Deliver it. Learn what actually matters to them. Then update the calculator.
